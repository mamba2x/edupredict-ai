ABSTRACT
This study investigates the influence of social media usage on the mental health of adolescents, with a particular focus on anxiety and depression. In light of the growing prevalence of social media engagement among young people, this research aims to explore how the duration and nature of social media interaction relate to psychological well-being. A descriptive survey research design was employed, involving 200 adolescents aged 13 to 19 from selected secondary schools and youth centers. Data were collected using a structured questionnaire comprising sections on demographic information, social media usage patterns, and mental health assessments using standardized tools (GAD-7 and PHQ-9). Findings revealed that a significant proportion of adolescents spend between 2 to over 6 hours daily on platforms such as Instagram, TikTok, and Snapchat. Analysis using the Statistical Package for Social Sciences (SPSS) showed that 55% and 54% of respondents experienced moderate to severe levels of anxiety and depression, respectively. Pearson correlation analysis indicated a strong positive relationship between time spent on social media and both anxiety (r = 0.62) and depression (r = 0.58), suggesting that increased social media use is associated with higher levels of psychological distress. The study underscores the potential mental health risks associated with excessive social media use and calls for increased awareness, digital literacy, and supportive interventions among adolescents and caregivers.
Keywords: Social media, Adolescents, Mental health, Anxiety, Depression, Digital behaviour

2.2	Theoretical Framework
The Theoretical Framework section discusses the theories or models guiding the research. This part may include social, psychological, or technological theories relevant to the topic. For example, consider the “theoretical framework section” below:
The Theoretical Framework is essential to grounding this study, providing a lens through which the research examines how social media impacts adolescent mental health. Theories such as Social Comparison Theory, Uses and Gratifications Theory, and Self-Determination Theory are pivotal in understanding the psychological and social drivers influencing social media use among adolescents, as well as the implications of these interactions.
Social Comparison Theory offers a critical perspective on how adolescents engage with social media. Developed by Festinger (1954), this theory suggests that individuals assess their self-worth by comparing themselves with others. On social media, adolescents are constantly exposed to carefully curated content that can create unrealistic standards of success, beauty, and happiness. They often perceive the curated images and positive updates on social media as genuine reflections of the lives of others, which can lead to feelings of inadequacy, low self-esteem, and increased anxiety (Vogel, Rose, Roberts & Eckles, 2014). Huang, (2017) highlights that these comparisons are intensified by the asynchronous, often superficial nature of social media interactions, where users typically present an idealized version of themselves. This effect is particularly pronounced on visual platforms like Instagram, where adolescents may feel inadequate when comparing themselves to idealized images (Pedalino & Camerini, 2022). According to social comparison theory, continuous exposure to these polished portrayals can substantially contribute to depressive symptoms among adolescents, as they may perceive their own lives as less fulfilling in comparison (Appel, Gerlach & Crusius, 2016). The theory thus underscores how frequent social comparisons can exacerbate feelings of depression and reduce self-esteem among adolescents (Burnell, George, Vollet, Ehrenreich & Underwood, 2019).
The Uses and Gratifications Theory provides insight into the psychological motivations that drive adolescents to engage with social media. This theory, articulated by Katz, Blumler and Gurevitch (1973), has evolved to reflect digital media’s changing the landscape, particularly social media. It posits that users are active agents seeking to satisfy various needs, such as entertainment, information, and social interaction. Recent studies have found that adolescents are often motivated to use social media to foster connections and gain validation from peers (Armstrong-Carter, Garrett, Nick, Prinstein & Telzer, 2022). However, these motivations can backfire, leading to potential mental health issues when expectations are not met or when they encounter negative interactions (Cauberghe, Van Wesenbeeck, De Jans, Hudders & Ponnet, 2021). The theory helps clarify how social media fulfils adolescents’ needs for belonging and acceptance, while simultaneously increasing their vulnerability to negative feedback and social isolation (Kelly, Zilanawala, Booker & Sacker, 2018).
Furthermore, Self-Determination Theory (SDT), introduced by Ryan and Deci (1985), explores the intrinsic and extrinsic motivations influencing behaviours, positing that individuals have fundamental needs for competence, autonomy, and relatedness. Social media can satisfy these needs by enabling adolescents to express themselves, connect with others, and achieve a sense of belonging (Choi & Lim, 2022). However, SDT also highlights that when these needs are thwarted, such as when adolescents feel pressured to conform to societal standards or when they encounter cyberbullying, their mental well-being may suffer (Ahn & Shin, 2023). This aspect is particularly pertinent as online interactions can undermine the authenticity of relationships, impacting adolescents’ self-worth and satisfaction (Orben, 2020).
Social Identity Theory is another framework relevant to this study, as it explains how individuals derive part of their identity from the social groups they belong to. Adolescents on social media often engage in identity exploration, influenced by the perceived norms and values of the online communities they interact with (Smith & Anderson, 2022). This theory suggests that while social media can foster positive identity development, it may also pressure adolescents to adopt identities that align with group norms, potentially leading to stress and a loss of individuality (Verduyn et al., 2020).
These theories provide a robust framework for analysing the complex interactions between adolescents and social media, helping to unpack the varied psychological effects that arise from social media use. They underscore how both the motivations for using social media and the types of interactions adolescents have online contribute to mental health outcomes, laying a foundation for understanding the broader implications of adolescent social media use.
2.3	Social Media Usage Among Adolescents
Social media has become an indispensable part of the daily lives of adolescents, profoundly shaping their communication, entertainment, and self-expression. Adolescents use social media for various purposes, such as maintaining relationships, discovering new trends, and exploring their identities. This section explores the extent, patterns, and implications of adolescent social media usage, supported by recent research.
2.3.1	Prevalence and Popular Platforms
Recent studies highlight the ubiquity of social media among adolescents. According to the Pew Research Center (2023), 95% of adolescents in the United States aged 13 to 17 have access to smartphones, with 89% actively engaging with at least one social media platform. Among these, YouTube emerges as the most widely used platform, engaging 95% of teens, followed by TikTok (67%), Instagram (62%), and Snapchat (59%). These platforms cater to different preferences, with YouTube serving as a hub for video content, TikTok focusing on short creative clips, and Instagram and Snapchat emphasizing visual storytelling and communication.
2.3.2	Patterns of Usage
Adolescents' engagement with social media is frequent and sustained. On average, teens spend 4.8 hours daily on these platforms, with higher usage during weekends and school holidays (Statista, 2024). This time is often divided among various activities such as:
Socializing: Teens frequently use platforms to communicate with peers via direct messages, comments, or video calls.
Content Creation: Many adolescents actively participate by posting images, videos, or stories to showcase their lives and creative abilities.
Information Seeking: Platforms like YouTube and TikTok are increasingly used to access information, tutorials, and news updates.
Entertainment: Adolescents consume entertainment content, including memes, music, and trending videos.
Adolescents tend to engage more with platforms that allow personalized experiences, such as tailored feeds and algorithm-driven recommendations, which keep them actively engaged for extended periods (Twenge & Campbell, 2018).
2.3.3	Factors Influencing Usage
Several factors contribute to adolescents' patterns of social media use:
Peer Influence: Social validation through likes, comments, and shares often motivates adolescents to remain active on social media (Verduyn, Gugushvili & Kross, 2022).
FOMO (Fear of Missing Out): The need to stay updated with peers’ activities and trends drives frequent social media checks (Statista, 2024).
Identity Exploration: Adolescents use social media as a space to experiment with their identities and gain feedback from peers, shaping their sense of self (Choi & Lim, 2022).
2.3.4	Benefits of Social Media Usage
Despite concerns about the negative impacts, social media also offers several benefits:
Enhanced Connectivity: Platforms enable adolescents to maintain friendships, especially during life transitions like changing schools.
Access to Support Networks: Social media provides spaces where adolescents can seek advice, share experiences, and engage with supportive communities, particularly for marginalized groups (Pew Research Center, 2023).
Learning Opportunities: Social media can serve as a platform for educational content, skill development, and exposure to diverse perspectives.
2.3.5	Risks Associated with Social Media Usage
The following are the ways excessive social media use can adversely affect the mental health of adolescents:
Social Comparison: Viewing curated, idealized content from peers often leads to feelings of inadequacy and low self-esteem (Verduyn et al., 2020).
Cyberbullying: Negative interactions, such as bullying and harassment, are prevalent risks, potentially leading to increased anxiety and depression (Twenge & Campbell, 2018).
Sleep Disruption: Late-night scrolling and exposure to blue light can disturb adolescents' sleep cycles, further exacerbating mental health issues (Statista, 2024).
2.3.6	Implications for Mental Health
The relationship between social media use and adolescent mental health is complex. While some adolescents benefit from positive reinforcement and community building, others face challenges such as anxiety, depression, and stress due to the pressures of online interaction (Twenge & Campbell, 2018). Therefore, understanding usage patterns and their implications is essential for developing strategies to mitigate risks while enhancing the benefits of social media for adolescents.
2.4	Psychological Impacts of Social Media
Social media has fundamentally transformed the way individuals interact, particularly among adolescents, shaping their psychological experiences and mental health. While these platforms provide opportunities for self-expression and connectivity, they also expose adolescents to challenges that can influence their psychological well-being. This section explores both the positive and negative psychological impacts of social media, supported by research evidence.
2.4.1	Positive Psychological Impacts
Despite concerns about the adverse effects of social media, its use can yield several psychological benefits, especially when used in moderation and within supportive contexts.
Enhancement of Social Connections: Social media enables adolescents to maintain and strengthen relationships, even with friends and family members who are geographically distant. This connectivity fosters a sense of belonging and support, particularly for individuals who might feel isolated in offline settings (Ellison et al., 2020). 
Opportunities for Self-Expression: Adolescents often use social media as a medium to express their thoughts, emotions, and identities. Platforms like TikTok and Instagram allow users to share creative content, which can enhance self-confidence and a sense of achievement (Choi & Lim, 2022). Adolescents engaged in content creation often report feelings of accomplishment and pride.
Access to Mental Health Resources: Social media can serve as a gateway to mental health support, offering adolescents information about coping strategies, professional resources, and peer support groups. Platforms like YouTube host educational content on topics such as stress management and mindfulness, contributing positively to emotional regulation (Twenge & Campbell, 2018).

RESEARCH METHODOLOGY
3.1	Preamble
This chapter outlines the research methodology adopted for the study. It also addresses issues of validity, reliability, and ethical considerations that guide the conduct of the research. The chosen methodology is aimed at ensuring the reliability and credibility of the findings concerning the influence of social media on adolescent mental health, particularly anxiety and depression.
3.2	Research Design
This study adopts a descriptive survey research design, which is appropriate for obtaining quantitative data regarding the influence of social media on the mental health of adolescents. The design enables the collection of relevant data from a large number of respondents within a short period. This approach facilitates an in-depth analysis of the relationship between social media usage and mental health indicators such as anxiety and depression among adolescents.
3.3	Population of the Study
The population of this study comprises adolescents aged between 13 and 19 years who are enrolled in selected public and private secondary schools as well as those who attend organized programs at youth centers. These institutions are situated within urban and semi-urban areas to ensure a broad demographic and socio-cultural representation of adolescents who are active users of social media platforms.
3.4	Sample and Sampling Technique
A total sample size of 200 adolescents will be selected for the study. The stratified random sampling technique will be utilized to ensure that the sample accurately represents key demographic variables such as gender, age group (13–15, 16–17, and 18–19 years), and socio-economic status (low, middle, and high income). Stratification ensures that all subgroups are adequately represented, and random selection within each stratum guarantees fairness and reduces selection bias.
3.5	Instrument for Data Collection
The primary instrument for data collection will be a structured, self-administered questionnaire. The questionnaire will be divided into three major sections:
Section A: Demographic Information, including age, gender, school type, and socio-economic background.
Section B: Social Media Usage Patterns covering frequency of use, duration, preferred platforms, and types of activities performed.
Section C: Mental Health Assessment, comprising standardized and validated tools such as the Generalized Anxiety Disorder-7 (GAD-7) scale for measuring anxiety levels and the Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) for assessing symptoms of depression.
3.6	Validity and Reliability of the Instrument
To ensure content and face validity, the questionnaire will be subjected to expert review by professionals in the fields of psychology, educational research, and adolescent health. Their feedback will be used to refine the items for clarity, relevance, and appropriateness. A pilot study will be conducted with 20 adolescents from a non-sampled school to test the reliability of the instrument. Internal consistency reliability will be determined using Cronbach's alpha coefficient, with a threshold of 0.70 considered acceptable for research instruments.
3.7	Method of Data Collection
The data collection process will involve the physical administration of questionnaires to the selected schools and youth centers. Before administration, consent forms will be distributed to participants and their parents or legal guardians, as applicable. Trained research assistants will guide respondents during the completion of the questionnaires to ensure understanding and accuracy. Data will be collected over a period of two weeks to allow flexibility and accommodate school schedules.
3.8	Method of Data Analysis
The collected data will be organized and analyzed using the Statistical Package for Social Sciences (SPSS). Descriptive statistics such as frequencies, percentages, means, and standard deviations will be used to summarize respondents’ demographic characteristics and social media usage. Inferential statistics, including Pearson’s correlation coefficient, will be used to determine the strength and direction of relationships between social media use and mental health indicators. Multiple regression analysis will be employed to predict the impact of various aspects of social media usage on anxiety and depression scores.
3.9	Ethical Considerations
This study will strictly adhere to ethical standards in research involving human participants. Informed consent will be obtained from all respondents and their guardians (where necessary) before participation. Confidentiality of participants’ information will be maintained, and responses will be used strictly for research purposes. The right to decline participation or withdraw from the study at any point without penalty will be clearly communicated. Additionally, participants who exhibit signs of distress during the study will be referred to appropriate counseling services.

4.6	Discussion of Findings
The results of this study align with previous research that identifies excessive social media use as a significant risk factor for poor mental health outcomes in adolescents. The high levels of anxiety and depression observed in this study are consistent with studies by Twenge et al. (2017) and Keles et al. (2020), which found similar associations. Several factors may explain this correlation:
Social Comparison: Adolescents frequently compare themselves to idealized images posted by peers and influencers, leading to reduced self-esteem.
Cyberbullying: Some respondents reported experiences with online harassment, which is known to be associated with emotional trauma.
Disrupted Sleep: Many adolescents engage in nighttime browsing, disrupting their sleep cycles and contributing to mood disorders.
Addiction and Validation Seeking: The compulsive need to check notifications and gain social approval can lead to stress and a sense of inadequacy.
While not all social media use is harmful, the patterns revealed in this study suggest that when use becomes excessive or emotionally driven, it can significantly impact an adolescent’s mental health. These findings provide a foundation for policy recommendations and interventions to support youth well-being in the digital age.
CHAPTER FIVE
SUMMARY, CONCLUSION, AND RECOMMENDATIONS
5.1	Summary 
This study investigated the impact of social media usage on the mental health of adolescents, focusing specifically on symptoms of anxiety and depression. The findings revealed a strong and statistically significant relationship between the frequency and intensity of social media engagement and the prevalence of anxiety and depressive symptoms among adolescents.
Adolescents who reported higher levels of social media use, measured in terms of time spent online, frequency of checking platforms, and emotional investment, tended to exhibit increased levels of restlessness, worry, sadness, low self-esteem, and social withdrawal. The study also noted that adolescents exposed to cyberbullying, social comparison, and validation-seeking behaviors through social media platforms were more likely to experience heightened psychological distress.
While some positive aspects of social media were acknowledged, such as increased social connection, access to information, and self-expression, the data clearly showed that excessive or unregulated use can contribute to emotional instability and negative mental health outcomes.
5.2	Recommendations
In light of the findings, the following recommendations are proposed:
Parental and Educational Guidance: Parents, teachers, and guardians should play an active role in monitoring and guiding adolescents' use of social media. Setting healthy boundaries, encouraging digital literacy, and fostering open conversations about online experiences can help mitigate the negative impacts.
Implementation of Mental Health Programs: Schools should incorporate mental health education into their curricula. Workshops, peer support groups, and counseling services should be made accessible to students to help them manage stress, build resilience, and recognize early signs of mental health issues.
Social Media Platform Responsibility: Social media companies should take greater responsibility in creating safer online environments. This includes developing features that promote well-being, such as time management reminders, mental health resources, content moderation, and support tools for users who may be experiencing psychological distress.
Encouragement of Offline Engagement: Encouraging adolescents to participate in offline activities such as sports, arts, community service, and family time can provide balance and reduce dependency on digital validation.
5.3	Limitations of the Study
This section acknowledges the constraints faced by the study, such as time, resources, or methodological limitations. For example, consider the limitations of the study below:
The research was limited to a sample of 200 adolescents from selected schools and youth centers, which may not fully capture the diversity of adolescents in different regions or socio-cultural settings. The reliance on self-reported data through questionnaires also presents a potential for bias, as respondents may have underreported or exaggerated their social media use or emotional experiences. Additionally, the cross-sectional nature of the study restricts the ability to determine causality between social media usage and mental health outcomes such as anxiety and depression. Although standardized screening tools like the Generalized Anxiety Disorder-7 (GAD-7) and Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) were used, they do not replace clinical diagnoses, thus limiting the depth of mental health assessment.
5.4	Conclusion
The findings of this research underscore the influential role that social media plays in shaping the mental well-being of adolescents. As a double-edged sword, social media offers numerous advantages in terms of communication, creativity, and connection. However, the negative consequences, especially in relation to anxiety and depression, cannot be ignored.
The study concludes that the persistent and unmoderated use of social media has a detrimental effect on adolescent mental health, exacerbating feelings of inadequacy, loneliness, and emotional vulnerability. The psychological toll is particularly severe when adolescents are constantly exposed to idealized representations of others, online harassment, or a lack of real-life social support.
Therefore, the influence of social media on adolescent anxiety and depression is both significant and complex, demanding proactive strategies from parents, educators, policymakers, and social media companies.
