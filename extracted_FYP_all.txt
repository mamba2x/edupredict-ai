140:        INTRODUCTION
141: Background Of Study
142: Student academic performance prediction has emerged as a burning issue of concern to educational institutions around the world in an effort to boost student success and the effectiveness of a given institution. as highlighted in recent reviews on EDM for retention (Romero & Ventura, 2020).Manual evaluation of student progress through traditional means of monitoring and evaluation is cumbersome in terms of scale and accuracy Hellas et al. (2018). High accuracy of predicting student performance is vital in identifying the student having problems in the academic process in the early stages of their learning process Belachew & Gobena (2017). Schools have a complicated problem of critically evaluating the achievements of students, finding the specific trends, and creating the approaches to their further academic development and achievement Francis & Babu (2021); Yousafzai et al. (2021). It becomes more challenging to manually track academic records and predict outcomes of students as the student populations are increasing, more so because each student may need a unique approach to improve their academic results Hellas et al. (2018). 
143: New opportunities of solving these challenges have appeared in educational contexts due to the development of machine learning technologies. Machine learning methods are able to find patterns and models in the educational data and give significant information in decision making Belachew & Gobena, (2017). Such data-driven methods allow to explore huge volumes of education data in a variety of perspectives, derive useful correlations and forecasts that could not be found by hand Waheed et al. (2020). , enabling proactive interventions (Uçar et al., 2022). 
144: Recent studies have shown that student performance prediction systems based on machine learning can obtain high accuracy rates, and some of them report accuracy as high as 90.57% in terms of ensemble Hasan et al (2021); Alomari et al. (2023).Moreover, these systems will be able to integrate various variables outside of the traditional academic performance, such as demographic data, personality traits, and psychographic ones, to make more precise and detailed projections Amrieh et al. (2016); Waheed et al. (2020). 
145: The importance of this field of research is not just limited to the accuracy of prediction but because of these systems, strategies of early intervention can be developed, personalized learning techniques are built, and better allocations of resources in educational establishments can be achieved which leads to better student achievement and performance of the institutions.
146: Statement of the Problem
147: Academic performance has a big problem facing educational institutions of the world that may need to anticipate and manage effectively these student performances directly affects the performance of every student and the overall effectiveness of an institution. The manual nature of the conventional student-progress monitoring system fails to help in the identification of at-risk students promptly and in most cases, the intervention may be implemented too late to stop any academic failure Belachew & Gobena (2017); Francis & Babu (2021); Yousafzai et al. (2021). Teachers can hardly keep track of and analyze academic records of high numbers of students manually, especially when a single student might need a different approach to enhance their performance Hellas et al.(2018). 
148: Moreover, a significant number of learning organizations do not have appropriate systematic methods of student performance monitoring and analysis, and the evaluation standards are not consistent between various institutions Asif et al. (2017)., exacerbating resource inefficiencies (Villano et al., 2018). Current prediction tools mainly focus on previous academic results only, not taking into account other factors, which are equally significant to student performance, which include demographics, characteristics of personality, and psychographics Amrieh et al. (2016); Waheed et al. (2020). 
149: This is due to the lack of effective early warning systems and therefore institutions are unable to offer timely counseling, further training, or special interventions to the at-risk students before they start performing poorly in academics Hasan et al (2021); Alomari et al. (2023).The sum of these related issues is increased dropout, decreased student satisfaction, poor resource allocation, and low institutional performance, necessitating coordinated, empirical solutions that would allow predicting student performance with established accuracy and respond to educational intervention proactively.
150: Aim and Objectives
151: Aim:
152: To develop and evaluate a machine learning-based academic performance prediction system using real secondary educational data in order to support informed academic monitoring and student-support decision-making.
153: Objectives:
154: To obtain and prepare real secondary educational performance data containing academic, demographic, social and school-related attributes for predictive analysis.
155: To examine the attributes represented in the Student Performance dataset and prepare them appropriately for machine learning model development.
156: To develop and compare candidate machine learning models for predicting student academic performance outcomes.
157: To evaluate the developed models using performance measures appropriate to the final prediction outcome implemented in the system.
158: To develop a web-based prototype that enables users to submit relevant student attributes and obtain an academic performance prediction output.
159: To discuss the ethical, privacy and contextual limitations associated with applying machine learning to student-performance prediction.
160: Methodology
161: This study adopted a quantitative predictive research design and a system development approach for the development of EduPredict AI. The study used the Student Performance dataset obtained from the UCI Machine Learning Repository. The dataset was originally compiled from school reports and questionnaires relating to students in two Portuguese secondary schools and was made available for academic performance prediction research (Cortez, 2008; Cortez & Silva, 2008).
162: Two subject-specific files were used in the study: student-mat.csv, containing 395 Mathematics records, and student-por.csv, containing 649 Portuguese language records. These files were combined to produce a working dataset of 1,044 student-subject performance records. The combined total was treated as subject-performance records rather than verified unique students because a student who studied both subjects may appear in both source files.
163: The dataset contained demographic, family, social, school-related and academic attributes. These included variables such as sex, age, parental educational background, study time, previous failures, school support, family support, extracurricular activity participation, internet access, absences and academic grades. The original academic outcome variable was G3, which represented the final subject grade on a scale of 0 to 20, while G1 and G2 represented earlier grading-period outcomes in the same subject.
164: The dataset was prepared for predictive analysis through appropriate inspection and transformation of its attributes. Categorical variables were converted into numerical representations suitable for machine learning, while scale-sensitive numerical predictors were standardised through a training-fitted scaling procedure where required. The data were divided into training and testing subsets using an 80:20 holdout strategy, producing 835 records for training and 209 records for testing. The testing subset was reserved for evaluating predictive performance on records not used during model training.
165: Candidate machine learning approaches considered for the system included Logistic Regression, Support Vector Machine, Gradient Boosting Classifier and Random Forest Classifier. These algorithms were selected to provide a comparison between an interpretable linear approach, a margin-based classifier and ensemble-learning approaches. Evaluation was carried out using measures appropriate to the final implemented prediction outcome. Where the outcome was represented categorically, suitable classification metrics included accuracy, precision, recall and F1-score; where the outcome was retained as a numerical grade, suitable regression measures included Mean Absolute Error, Root Mean Squared Error and the coefficient of determination.
166: The web-based prototype was designed to integrate the trained prediction pipeline into an operational decision-support interface. The frontend component enabled users to submit relevant student information, while the backend processed submitted values using the same transformations applied during model development and returned the prediction output. The system was intended to support academic review and student assistance rather than to serve as an autonomous basis for consequential academic decisions.

220: 2.2.1 Student Academic Performance
221: Student academic performance is the quantifiable results of learning activity of a student and it is a highly essential measure of learning efficiency and personal achievement., encompassing cognitive, behavioral, and emotional dimensions (Zhang et al., 2021). In predictive systems that are based on machine learning, academic performance goes beyond the conventional grade-based systems to encompass holistic assessment of cognitive, behavior and developmental performance. 
222: Belachew and Gobena (2017). made use of such exhaustive measures as final GPA and grades in all courses as main measures of academic performance, whereas Waheed et al (2020)., and Asif et al. (2017). have underlined that the assessment of performance should also take into consideration the personality factors, interests, attributes, and opinions which have a significant impact on academic performance in particular. These traditional measures are based on the quantitative indices of grades, test scores, and course completion rates, and Kabakus & Kara (2022). have obtained prediction accuracy of 96.7% based on these indicators., highlighting the role of quantitative indices in model training (Ahmed, 2024). 
223: It has been determined that the variables that affect academic performance are many, among them are the demographic factors, education levels of parents, behavioral factors such as time of study and attendance, and environmental factors. Waheed et al. (2020). also presented the importance of external variables like geographical location, parental education, and health condition in shaping academic performance. 
224: In predictive modeling scenarios, academic performance is both the variable to forecast and the variable used to build the model, and Francis & Babu (2021); Yousafzai et al. (2021). show that machine learning models are capable of predicting the performance of students with an accuracy of 84%., with recent advancements in feature engineering pushing accuracies even higher (Albreiki et al., 2024). The performance is usually categorized in either pass/fail or excellent/good/average/poor categories which allow the assessment as well as the prediction. 
225: The temporal nature of academic performance permits early warning mechanisms that can be used to identify the students at risk at a very young age and take necessary interventions through tutoring courses, academic coaching, and special needs services, which can go a long way in enhancing the future performance of the students and the educational institutions.

245: 2.2.2 Educational Data Mining
246: Educational Data Mining is a sub-specialty that uses data mining methods on educational data to identify patterns, relationships, and insights that can be used in educational decision-making and to enhance learning outcomes. It is an interdisciplinary discipline that incorporates algorithms of machine learning, statistics and educational research to study massive datasets produced by education institutions and learning systems.
247: As Alyahyan & Düştegör (2020). highlighted the tools of data mining and machine learning, it has become less problematic to examine the educational data in various angles and obtain diverse information that can lead students to their future success and assist them in discovering their areas of interest based on their abilities and potentials, fostering personalized pathways (Namoun & Alshanqiti, 2021).
248: Educational data mining involves the methodical examination of academic histories, demographic data, behavioral trends, and learning interactions of students to determine what influences academic achievement and predetermines the performance results in the future. Belachew and Gobena (2017). applied educational data mining practically by using student transcript data of Wolkite University that covers several years and found models and patterns that would be useful in educational decision-making.
249: The subject matter has changed to integrate various data levels other than the traditional academic records, as shown by Waheed et al (2020)., and Asif et al. (2017)., educational data mining can incorporate personality factors, interests, attributes, and opinions in addition to demographic and academic data to offer a holistic view on the learning patterns of students.
250: The principal methodological basis of educational data mining is machine learning techniques, and Hasan et al (2021); Alomari et al. (2023). show how educational establishments can apply such techniques to scan and notify the students about risky behavior, further counseling or training them on the basis of the collected data.
251: Educational data mining has also been used in other functions of an institution such as predicting student performance, early warning systems, optimization of resource allocation, and individual learning suggestions. Francis & Babu (2021); Yousafzai et al. (2021). noted that educational data mining allows institutions to establish the performance of students on a high level of accuracy, which allows early detection of the students who might perform poorly as well as implement proactive intervention measures.
252: Its increasing accessibility of educational data and technology has enabled more effective execution of educational data mining to be more accessible, with the article by Kabakus & Kara (2022). with the use of extensive datasets consisting of thousands of student records illustrating how data mining methods can attain an impressive level of prediction accuracy of up to 96.7%.
253: Educational data mining also includes the correlation analysis of many variables on the performance of students and studies have identified the relationship between academic performance and such variables as study time, parental education, socioeconomic factors, and behavioral pattern.
254: Its development remains dynamic with further incorporation of sophisticated analytical procedures and the fact that educational data mining has the capabilities of offering insightful information to enhance teaching strategies, curriculum development, student care, and all organizational efficiency to enhance student achievements and quality education. A widely cited review of more than 120 studies confirms that feature selection and ensemble methods remain the most dominant approaches in educational data mining (Shahiri et al., 2023).
255: 2.2.3 Learning Analytic
256: Learning Analytics is the systemized checking of data concerning learners and their surroundings to gain knowledge and maximize the learning processes and educational settings as they arise. The domain exploits the more sophisticated computational methods and machine learning procedures to convert raw educational information into a form of actionable information that can improve student learning, teaching, and institutional decision-making efforts. Hellas et al. (2018)., and highlighted that learning analytics can be used to develop machine learning tools that may forecast future academic performance and contribute to high performance of a student at all levels of his/her educational progress as teachers and faculty members will have the capability to systematically track and analyze the progress of a student, as evidenced in blended learning contexts (Wang & Yu, 2025).
257: Learning analytics is a broad concept of analysis of learning behaviour, student engagement trends, student academic performance, and other contextual aspects that affect the outcomes of the learning process. Waheed et al (2020)., and Asif et al. (2017). revealed that learning analytics could integrate various data types, such as academic history, demographic and behavioral data, and psychographic variables to create a complete profile of a student to create personalized learning strategies and specific interventions. Its techniques include advanced forms of analysis such as machine learning algorithms, statistical modeling, and predictive analytics to find patterns and relationships in educational data that might not be obvious using other traditional methods of assessment.
258: Alyahyan & Düştegör (2020). highlighted the ability of learning analytics to give insights about the preferred teaching strategies, learning potential, and academic performance trends in students, allowing the educator to alter their teaching models according to the needs of specific students and maximise learning experiences.
259: Early warning systems and learning analytics are equally important to identify students who are prone to failing academically before the problem is too big to handle, as Belachew and Gobena (2017). prove that analytical methods can accurately predict the performance outcome of students and therefore allow providing timely interventions and support services.
260: Beyond curriculum design, resource allocation, student support service, and institutional planning, the empirical applications of learning analytics have extended to other education-related domains such as the placement prediction system, which Kabakus & Kara (2022). suggest to use the analytical insights to guide students and institutions to make sound career choices and academic specialization. Learning analytics also include real time monitoring and feedback systems, which furnish real time information on the advancement of student learning, and thus, can facilitate dynamic learning environments, which dynamically react to individual student needs and learning trends.
261: Francis & Babu (2021); Yousafzai et al. (2021). emphasized that learning analytics can yield high prediction accuracy in predicting student outcomes to allow educational institutions to adopt data-driven approaches to improving the percentage of students that succeed and overall education effectiveness.
262: By linking learning analytics to institutional systems and processes, it will be possible to provide the holistic management of students, starting with admission and enrollment, continuing with graduation and professional placement, and offering sustained insights on student performance and institutional growth. Hasan et al (2021); Alomari et al. (2023). have shown that superior analytical algorithms such as ensemble algorithms can deliver superior results in learning analytics usage and allow generating more accurate and dependable results about student learning behavior and performance forecasting.
263: As a revolutionary method of education, learning analytics will place the institutions in a focus of action rather than reaction and promote the use of evidence-driven decision-making processes that will support the students, educators, and institutions by enhancing the outcomes of the learning process, improving the teaching process, and helping the institutions to optimize the use of the available resources.
264: 2.2.4 Academic Performance Indicators
265: Academic Performance Indicators are quantified variables used to evaluate, track, and forecast student academic achievement in machine-learning-based prediction systems. These indicators supply the core data that allow algorithms to detect patterns, make predictions, and provide insights for educational stakeholders. Conventional indicators include grades, test scores, GPA, and course completion rates, Belachew and Gobena (2017). using final GPA and course grades in their prediction model at Wolkite University. Modern approaches extend beyond these traditional metrics to incorporate broader variables that influence performance.
266: Kabakus & Kara (2022). showed that placement performance, combined with detailed academic history, can yield prediction accuracy up to 96.7%. Contemporary indicator frameworks also integrate demographic factors such as age, gender, family background, parental education, and socioeconomic status, with Waheed et al (2020)., and Asif et al. (2017). using 33 such variables to achieve 93.8% accuracy further enhanced by multi-model fusion approaches that incorporate demographic data for improved prediction (Zou et al., 2025). Behavioral indicators like study time, attendance, extracurricular involvement, and engagement levels have also become essential, as balanced study–social patterns correlate with higher achievement.
267: External factors; including geographical location, parental education, health, and internet access must also be considered because they significantly influence academic success Waheed et al. (2020). . Psychographic variables such as interests, attitudes, opinions, personality traits, and learning preferences can further enhance predictive accuracy, as shown by Waheed et al. (2020). with their Interest, Attitude, and Opinion (IAO). variables. Time-based indicators, including improvement trends, consistency indices, and longitudinal performance patterns, help reveal student learning trajectories and future outcomes, which Alyahyan & Düştegör (2020). found useful for understanding learning styles, potential, and career alignment.
268: Social and environmental factors; family relationships, peer interactions, lifestyle habits, and after-school activities also play a significant role in forecasting achievement. Integrating multiple categories of indicators allows machine learning models to build complex student profiles that reflect the true breadth of factors influencing success, with Francis & Babu (2021); Yousafzai et al. (2021). achieving 84% accuracy using Support Vector Machines on diverse indicator sets. Institutional and contextual variables such as school quality, teacher effectiveness, resource availability, and learning environment are equally relevant. Hasan et al (2021); Alomari et al. 307: 2.4.1 Machine Learning (ML)
308: Machine Learning is a sub-discipline of artificial intelligence that is concerned with creating algorithms and systems that can learn to forecast and discern patterns based on the data supplied to them without being directly coded to accomplish any particular task. According to Waheed et al (2020)., and Asif et al. (2017)., integrating supervised techniques for robust outcomes (Sanfo, 2025). the process of machine learning is oriented to the understanding of the complexity of different kinds of the collected data and the selection of the right model on the basis of the data by attempting a variety of models, which is fundamentally different to the simple computing algorithms to solve the problem.
309: Machine learning algorithms in the context of education are developed to assess the inputs of data, develop training sets, and generate the necessary outputs with the help of statistical methods of estimation that allow calculating the systems on the educational data patterns and make accurate predictions regarding the final outcomes of students. Belachew and Gobena (2017). underlined that teaching and learning data is analyzed by machine learning methods to reveal the models or patterns, and it is especially useful in the case of educational facilities that need to increase the level of student success and optimize the allocation of resources.
310: Machine learning has been applied to student performance prediction, and these algorithms rely on supervised learning techniques including Support Vector Machine, Neural Networks, Decision Trees, Logistic Regression, and K-Nearest Neighbor algorithms, with Francis & Babu (2021); Yousafzai et al. (2021). proving that all of these algorithms can be effectively used to predict student academic results with high levels of accuracy. The state of the machine learning applications involve ensemble techniques, which involve using more than one different algorithm and can be used to obtain better accuracy rates of 90.57% in predicting student performance using ensemble techniques of Support Vector machine, Decision Tree and K-Nearest Neighbor algorithms Hasan et al (2021); Alomari et al. (2023).
311: The educational application of machine learning systems takes in various types of data, such as academic records, demographic information, behavioral patterns and psychographic variables with Kabakus & Kara (2022). recording exceptional prediction rates of 96.7% when multilayer perceptron neural networks worked on comprehensive student datasets.
312: Machine learning works the other way: training algorithms are trained using historic data of students to determine patterns and relations that can be useful later to predict outcomes, and Alyahyan & Düştegör (2020). indicate how both prediction supervised learning methods and unsupervised learning algorithms such as K-means clustering can be combined to offer detailed information to student performance trends and groupings. (2020). depicted the idea of how machine learning is capable of merging multidimensional data of complex nature such as interests, attitudes, opinions, demographics, and behavioral patterns to deliver a high rate of prediction along with a detailed insight into the factors that impact student success.

The ability of machine learning to process large scale educational data and the presence of hidden patterns that might not be easily visible using alternative analytical tools enables such program to be especially useful to current educational organizations that are interested in maximizing student success rates and institutional performance.

316: 2.4.2 Supervised Learning
317: Supervised Learning is a machine learning paradigm, based on the principle of using labeled training data, to construct predictive models that are able to make accurate predictions of new data, through the learning of input-output relationships, induced in the course of training. Supervised learning algorithms in student performance projections are trained using historical student data with both the input features and the desired outcome known, so that a model can be developed that can correctly predict future student outcome with great accuracy. Belachew and Gobena (2017). showed what neural networks, Naive Bayesian, and Support Vector Machine can do to supervised learning, whereas Kabakus & Kara (2022). managed to reach prediction rates of 96.7% with the help of various supervised learning methods such as multilayer perceptron and logistic classifiers.

319: Classification is a supervised type of learning which predicts categorical results by categorizing input data into previously known classes or categories. Classification algorithms in educational environments forecast results of pass/fail or excellent/good/average/poor levels or placed/not placed in educational settings. Francis & Babu (2021); Yousafzai et al. (2021). illustrated classification with the Support Vector Machine to attain 84% accuracy in predicting the categories of student performance, whereas Hasan et al (2021); Alomari et al. 320: Regression
321: Regression is a supervised learning model that forecasts continuous numerical values by modeling mathematical connections amid input variables and target values. Regression algorithms in student performance prediction are used to predict individual numerical scores, GPA, or probability score to succeed in the academic performance. Linear Regression and KNN were used by Alyahyan & Düştegör (2020). to predict continuous student performance measurements and regression methods allow the institution to predict individual grades, test results, or percentages of student performance instead of discrete categories, which gives a finer view into anticipated academic outcomes of students and allows setting the threshold of intervention more accurately.

371: The following Pre-processing Techniques are commonly used in student performance prediction system;
372: Data Cleaning
373: Data cleaning is the process of determining and processing missing values, deleting the duplication of the records, and fixing the inconsistent data entries. According to Waheed et al (2020)., and Asif et al. (2017)., their data was so clean that they did not need to go through data cleaning, which is why it is necessary when there are problems with data quality in student records, crucial for handling missing values in EDM (Zaffar et al., 2021).

375: Label encoders convert the categorical variables into the numerical form like gender, type of schools, and the type of courses to ensure that they can be compatible with machine learning algorithms. In the study of Waheed et al. (2020)., the process of converting categorical variables into numerical values was performed using the tool of Label Encoder and allowed algorithms to handle non-numeric variables of the students.
376: Transformation and normalization of data.
377: Continuous variables are normalized or standardized which means that all the features equally contribute to the learning process and the variables with higher scales will not dominate the prediction model. This method will make sure that such features as age, GPA, and study hours will be positioned in similar scales to achieve better algorithm results.
378: Feature Selection
379: The feature selection methods are used to determine the most meaningful predictors of student performance through a correlation analysis and statistical analysis. Waheed et al. (2020). used the feature selection to extract the appropriate features out of their dataset of 33 variables, and correlation analysis to show that there were strong ties between performance of students and such factors as the education of their mother and the time they spent studying.
380: 2.4.5 Model Evaluation Metrics
381: To access accurate prediction, several evaluation metrics are used. The following are model evaluation used;
382: Accuracy
383: Accuracy is the proportion of correctly identified instances regarding all predictions by the model. Accuracies of up to 96.7% in MLP, 96% in other algorithms were obtained by Kabakus & Kara (2022). , and 90.57% with ensemble methods by Hasan et al (2021); Alomari et al. (2023)., proving the efficiency of various methods in predicting student performance , alongside F1-score for balanced evaluation (Sanfo, 2025).
384: Precision
385: Precision measures the percentage of accurate positive predictions of the model, meaning the percentage of the positive predictions that the model makes which are also accurate. This measurement plays an important role to determine the predictability of all those students who have a chance of succeeding in academics.
386: Recall
387: Recall can be used to determine the ratio of the number of actual positive predictions made by the model to the number of actual positive cases in the dataset and it indicates the level that the model can identify all students who will actually perform well. High recall is a factor that guarantees that the prediction system does not miss many of the high-performing students.
388: F-measure
389: F-measure is the harmonic average of precision and recall, which is a balanced measure of evaluation, and it takes into account false positives and false negatives. Kabakus & Kara (2022). have used the F-measure and other measures to examine the performance of the algorithm in student placement prediction in a comprehensive manner.
390: ROC Area
391: ROC (Receiver Operating Characteristic). Area is a measure of how the model can differentiate between dissimilar classes in varying threshold conditions. Kabakus & Kara (2022). incorporated the analysis of ROC areas into the framework of their comprehensive evaluation and obtained information on the model discrimination capacity.
392: True Positive Rate (TPR).
393: TPR or sensitivity is the ratio of the number of actual positive cases that are correctly determined by the model. High TPR in student performance prediction guarantees that majority of the students who are going to perform well are identified correctly and offered the relevant support and opportunities.

425: 3.2 Research Design
426: The research method used in this study was quantitative, predictive and system development. The quantitative component was appropriate since the study involved analysing structured student records with both numerical and categorical attributes and aimed to build a predictive model which is an academic outcome. Predictive research involves modelling the relationship between observable variables and a target variable of interest for new observations, and in the context of education, how academic, demographic, family, social and school-related factors are related to the student's performance (Hellas et al., 2018; Alyahyan & Düştegör, 2020). 
427: However, the study didn't end at model experimentation and the system development component was adopted. The trained predictive model was supposed to be accessible via a web interface called 'EduPredict AI' that was to be developed as a useful software prototype. 
428: In this approach, the research process included the following steps: Data set collection, Data set inspection, Outcome definition, Preprocessing, Model training, Evaluation, Model selection, Serialization and integration with front-end and back-end application. 
429: The deployed interface was designed as an academic support application, allowing users to populate student attributes with the aim of providing an output to be predicted which could then be used for further human review. 
430: Design not experimental: student characteristics were not manipulated by researcher, nor were interventions assigned. Rather, preexisting secondary education data was used to build a predictive model of pre-existing attributes and academic outcomes. As such, outputs from the models suggest statistical patterns of prediction, not causal evidence of any specific variable impacting student performance.
431: 3.2.1 Justification for Research Design
432: The research design is justified on the basis of the following arguments. For the following reasons a predictive research design was appropriate for this research. First, the Student Performance dataset was specially made available for regression and classification studies of student achievement in secondary education, which provides an appropriate empirical foundation for the development of the model (Cortez, 2008; Cortez and Silva, 2008). 
433: Secondly, the common approach of using student-performance prediction to identify students who might need support has been widely reported in the literature via historical student attributes and supervised machine learning methods (Hellas et al., 2018; Alyahyan & Düştegör, 2020). 
434: Third, incorporation of a chosen model into an application allows the research result to be beyond analysis and become a prototype of an educational decision support system. 
435: The design also allowed for a systematic comparison of various algorithms using the same analytical procedure. The same training data could be used to train a candidate model and the same testing data could be used to test the candidate models. In this way the fairness of algorithm comparison is enhanced, and it can be guaranteed that any differences between the algorithms are due to the modelling approach and not due to the evaluation sample.

The data was obtained from two secondary schools in Portugal, through the use of school reports and questionnaires (Cortez, 2008; Cortez & Silva, 2008). 
439: Thus, the population of the study was not undergraduate students of a tertiary institution in Nigeria, but secondary school learners who appeared in the Portuguese educational context. The population was sufficiently representative for the development of an academic-performance prediction prototype because the data included a wide variety of traits which can be used in predictive modelling such as study time, previous failures, school support, parental background, absences and grades.
440:  However, the dataset was also geographically, educationally and culturally constrained, with the implication that a model based off the data could not be automatically assumed to be accurate for the Nigerian university students and would require local validation, retraining or external testing with a more appropriate population.
441: 3.3.1 Dataset as Population Representation
442: The data set used for this study was UCI's Student Performance data set (Cortez, 2008). It includes data about the achievement of students in two subjects: Mathematics and Portuguese language. There are 395 records in the Mathematics file student-mat.csv and 649 records in the Portuguese language file student-por.csv. 
443: A working data set of 1,044 student-subject performance records was obtained by merging the two files, from which the model was developed. The total is not considered to be 1,044 verified unique students, but rather student-subject performance records. 
444: The UCI documentation states that students enrolled in both subjects may be listed in both source files and that attributes with unique values across both classes of students ("id across both") may be used to identify the students and to merge the two file sets (Cortez, 2008). 
445: Keeping the subject specific records makes the unit of prediction a student's result in a specific subject context. There are 30 predictor attributes and three course-grade attributes in the original data set. 
446: The original outcome variable is G3, and is scored on a 0 to 20 scale; G1 is the first period grade and G2 is the second period grade in the same subject. Because G1 and G2 are the earlier grades in the same course, using them can significantly influence prediction in the later grades of the course, G3; however, this is not an early entry warning model, as the model using the G1 and G2 variables is more of a mid-course, later-stage, performance estimator than an early entry warning model (Cortez, 2008; Cortez & Silva, 2008).

448: 3.3.2 Unit of Analysis and Generalisability
449: A record of a subject's performance was the unit of analysis for this study. Each record was the measurement of the characteristics and the recorded outcome of a student's performance in either Mathematics or Portuguese language.

453: 3.4 Sample and Sampling Technique
454: The entire merged working data set of 1044 student-subject performance records from the two source data sets were used for the study. No smaller sample was created because the study was secondary data and wanted to take advantage of all available real world information used in education to develop models. 
455: The full data set retained all the subject records and did not discard observations during the first phase of model building. In the field of machine learning, sampling mainly takes the place of splitting records into a portion for model fitting and an independent portion for evaluating the machine learning model. 
456: The methodology thus aimed to subset records into training and testing sets in a way that would leave the model with enough learning data but not overwhelm it with the records it hadn't encountered while training, while simultaneously leaving the observations it had seen during training for testing.

481: A holdout validation strategy was used to ensure that records for learning are held back for evaluation. With the 80:20 partition used in the study, 835 records were put into the training subset and 209 records were put into the testing subset. 
482: Pre-processing transformations and candidate prediction models were fit on the training subset, and the testing subset was used for the estimation of predictive performance for data that was not utilized in the fit of the model. 483: A random state of 42 was kept constant throughout the partitioning process to allow the same partitioning to be reproduced at any point the experimental procedure was repeated. This would facilitate a clear comparison of candidate models and aid in preventing the performance differences of the models being attributable to a shifting split.
484: 3.4.3 Sampling Method and Class Representation
485: The suitability of stratification will be dependent on the final operational form of the outcome variable. The original UCI outcome is a numerical grade for the final outcome G3. 
486: If the ultimate application includes splitting G3 into individual performance classes for use in a decision-support display, then the splitting of the classes should be stratified to ensure that each class is represented in the training and test sets. 
487: This is particularly crucial for situations where one group is comprised of students at risk, as overall accuracy can be misleading and poor identification of students who require intervention (He & Garcia, 2009). 
488: When the final model, instead, makes a continuous numerical prediction for G3, random holdout partitioning is suitable, provided that the distribution of the outcomes values is examined and reported. In both cases the outcome definition and the corresponding splitting procedure should be kept unchanged prior to model evaluation, to avoid a ‘post data-mining’ of the test data set.

634: 3.5.2 Outcome Variable and Operationalisation
635: The source data indicated the original outcome variable was G3, representing the subject grade (0-20) that was received at the end of the third grading period. This variable allows two formulating predictions. 
636: The model in a regression formulation is a model that estimates a numerical final grade. Documented grade thresholds can be used in a classification formulation to transform G3 into defined performance levels for alert and intervention decisions. 
637: According to the UCI repository, the dataset has also been used in a study for modelling and classification for the regression task (Cortez, 2008; Cortez & Silva, 2008). In an operational definition for the output, the meaning of this output must be clear and carefully described in the final implementation record, particularly when the output is part of a decision support application (e.g., EduPredict AI). 
638: If a performance category is shown, it should be accompanied by a specific G3 level, if applicable. This helps to ensure the interpretability of the system, helps with independent replication, and guards against performance labels being assigned without a clear educational foundation. Special notice too must be taken of the inclusion of G1 and G2.
639: These prior grades are valid predictors for predicting final course grades as soon as they are available, but using these prior grades makes it a later-stage predictor. If the objective is an early warning system, a separate model that incorporates data from the early warning period but does not incorporate data from later grading periods could be more useful to make an assessment of risk before academic problems have become apparent.
640: 3.6 Validity and Reliability of the Instrument
641: For this study, the validity and reliability of the secondary data and the predictive modelling procedure were taken into account. The data did not involve a questionnaire that the researcher designed, so validity was not confirmed by a new pilot instrument and Cronbach's alpha coefficient. Rather, focus was placed on the appropriateness of the attributes at their disposal for the goal of prediction, and the trustworthiness of the original data source and the integrity of the analysis pipeline.
642: 3.6.1 Dataset and Construct Validity
643: This was supplemented by the process of using a documented educational dataset obtained from genuine school reports and questionnaires which has been made publicly available for analysis of student performance (Cortez, 2008; Cortez & Silva, 2008). 
644: In addition to direct measures of academic achievement (G1, G2 and G3), there are contextual measures of study time, failure, support, absences and family-oriented factors. These types of attributes are consistent with the academic literature on prediction, which uses historical, behavioural and contextual data to analyse educational outcomes (e.g., Hellas et al., 2018; Alyahyan & Düştegör, 2020). 
645: Construct validity is determined by interpretation of the target. G3 is a numerical prediction of the final grade for the subject in the source context. If categories are assigned to G3, the definitions of the categories are part of the construct being measured and must be justified and documented. 
646: The model should therefore be described as predicting a recorded final-grade outcome or explicitly derived classification of performance, not general intelligence or ability, or long-term academic success.
647: 3.6.2 Reliability, Reproducibility and Threats to Validity
648: The modelling procedure was repeated using the same pipeline for data preparation, partitioning, model fitting and evaluation for all candidate models, which increases the reliability of the modelling procedure. No fitted transformations were applied to the training and testing subsets, and any scaling operations were fitted to the training data before being applied to the testing data. 
649: This learn-predict separation prevents leakage of information from the withheld data to the modelling phase (Kaufman et al., 2011). Reproducibility was achieved by fixing the random state in partition the data and by saving the trained model that was selected and the preprocessing artefacts that were fitted for future prediction. 
650: However, there were still some limitations to the validity. The data is from two secondary schools in Portugal, and so the findings are not generalizable to other national or tertiary-education settings. Students appearing in multiple subject records may have multiple records. G2 and G1 can also provide good predictions but can be less timely for intervention, as these grades are already predictors of current performance. 
651: These limitations do not make the prototype invalid, but rather specify the circumstances for which its results are valid. When considering any implementation for institutional use, representative local records, a well-defined prediction time point and documented fairness and privacy protections should be evaluated.
652: 3.6.3 Performance Evaluation Framework
653: The assessment system should be aligned with the outcome formulation that is operationalized in the final model. Classification metrics are suitable when G3 is mapped onto a finite number of performance classes, regression metrics suitable when the system's output is a continuous numerical G3. Choosing the metrics after seeing model outcomes would detract from the method's transparency and so the output form and evaluation measure should be determined prior to final model comparisons.

688: 3.7 Method of Data Collection and Data Preprocessing
689: In this study, secondary data were used from UCI Machine Learning Repository, rather than collecting new student data. The Student Performance dataset was chosen as this was gathered from real secondary school records and questionnaires and was designed for the analysis of educational achievement by data mining techniques (Cortez, 2008; Cortez & Silva, 2008).
690: For the prototype, the use of an established public dataset allowed for prototype development without regard to students' personally identifiable information in the intended deployment environment. 
691: The source dataset is a mixture of numerical, ordinal and categorical attributes, so data preprocessing was needed. Machine learning estimators need to have consistent representations of the input and a data pre-processing sequence is critical to prevent information leakage and to ensure that model evaluation is based on unseen records. The data processing method used in this research is described in the next subsections.
692: 3.7.1 Dataset Acquisition, Combination and Preliminary Inspection
693: The student will design and carry out surveys to gather, integrate, and analyze data for initial observation. The two data files (student-mat.csv and student-por.csv), both for the same subject, were downloaded from the UCI Machine Learning Repository and merged to form a working data set of 1,044 subject-performance records. 
694: The consistency of the file structures was queried before preparation of the models with regard to attribute names, data types, and definition of outcomes. According to the official repository, no values are missing from the dataset, so the initial task was to verify the structure of the data that was imported and to identify any mixture of categorical, ordinal and numerical variables that needed to be transformed (Cortez, 2008). 
695: The two subject files have the same attribute scheme but, respectively, they are related to different subject contexts. Any merging operation thus should keep a clear understanding that a record is a performance and not necessarily an individual. 
696: If duplicate-person analysis is needed, the attributes in the UCI that are required for identification should be considered, and not personal characteristics beyond the requirements of the study should be reconstructed.
697: 3.7.2 Encoding of Categorical and Ordinal Variables
698: There are binary categorical, nominal categorical and ordinally coded variables in the source. Before fitting machine learning estimators, non-numeric values need to be numerically encoded in a manner that is compatible with machine learning algorithm and does not encode the inappropriate relationships between categories. 
699: The scikit-learn library offers transformation utilities and supervised learning estimators that can be used to implement such pre-processing procedures (Pedregosa et al., 2011). Any binary variable like schoolsup, famsup, activities, internet and higher could be encoded numerically with a value of 0 or 1. For nominal variables (e.g. school, Mjob, Fjob, reason and guardian), a representation which does not imply a higher value of one category over another (such as one-hot) must be used. 
700: When a variable's documentation is ordinal and the ordinal representation is consistent with the repository description, ordered variables like Medu, Fedu, studytime, famrel, freetime, Dalc, Walc, health can be kept in the repository as such. Each encoder or feature mapping to be used in the training needs to be saved and used again without change when new data are pushed into the application during deployment.
701: 3.7.3 Feature Scaling and Prevention of Data Leakage
702: In cases where scale sensitive candidate models were fitted, the numerical predictor variables were scaled using a scaling procedure that was fitted using the training data. The typical way to standardise involves centring the variable around the mean of the training set, and scaling it by the standard deviation of the training set. 
703: This fitted transformation was only applied to the training subset and then applied to both the training and testing subsets; the testing subset was not allowed to feed into the transformation process. 
704: The procedure is crucial because if it is performed using full data set, information from held-out evaluation records can affect the model preparation, resulting in an overly optimistic prediction of the performance of the model. This is called leakage and Kaufman et al (2011) stress the importance of separating learning of the model from subsequent prediction or evaluation. 
705: The relevance of scaling is especially important in Logistic Regression and Support Vector Machine since their optimisation behaviour and decision surface can be influenced by the difference in scale of features. However, the same prepared data representation can be provided to the candidates which are based on trees so as to have a consistent comparative experiment.
706: 3.7.4 Feature Retention, Prediction Timing and Interpretability
707: The UCI data contains a wide range of candidate predictors, ranging from academic, social, family, school-related. Predicting should be used to inform a prediction purpose and feature retention should follow suit. G1 and G2 may be very useful for prediction of G3, but these are observed grades of the same subject, and may be less useful for very early intervention. Therefore, it is important that the final implementation clearly indicates whether it is for initial screening prior to posting of recorded grades or for support later in the year following recorded grades. 
708: If the model explanation is obtained with coefficient magnitudes/feature-importance values, then results should only be seen as evidence of use of the predictor by the fitted model, and not as evidence of a causal influence. 
709: Relationships in the training data might drive a variable to be included in a model prediction, but this doesn't mean that the variable's manipulation should result in a student's performance change. This is particularly relevant for sensitive or contextual characteristics like sex, family support, parental education, social variables etc.

749: In the present study, supervised machine learning was used on the created educational dataset for data analysis. Because an academic outcome, G3, was identified in the source dataset, it was appropriate to use supervised learning.

826: 3.9 Ethical Considerations
827: Ethical considerations were integral to the development of EduPredict AI because the system analyses student-related information and may influence academic support decisions. Learning analytics and educational prediction create responsibilities relating to privacy, transparency, student agency, profiling and the consequences of interpreting predictions about individuals (Slade & Prinsloo, 2013). In addition, predictive models can reproduce inequities contained in source data or generated through variable selection and decision-making practices (Baker & Hawn, 2022).
828: Accordingly, the system was positioned as a decision-support prototype rather than an autonomous decision-maker. A prediction may indicate that a student should be reviewed for supportive intervention, but it should not be used independently to impose academic penalties, deny opportunities or make irreversible decisions about a learner.
829: 3.9.1 Data Privacy and Confidentiality
830: The public Student Performance dataset does not contain direct identifiers such as student names, matriculation numbers or email addresses. Nevertheless, it contains personal and contextual attributes including sex, family background, social behaviour, health and alcohol-consumption indicators (Cortez, 2008). Such variables remain ethically sensitive even in a de-identified public dataset because their use in predictive classification may shape perceptions of a learner or influence institutional support decisions.
831: Where the deployed system is used with new student records, confidentiality must be protected through appropriate access controls, secure storage, restricted administrative access and a defined retention policy for prediction records. The prototype should therefore not be deployed with identifiable operational records until appropriate authentication, role-based access and institutional data-protection procedures have been established.
832: 3.9.2 Bias, Sensitive Variables and Fairness
833: The inclusion of demographic, family and social variables introduces a risk that a predictive model may reflect or reinforce disadvantages associated with student background. Educational algorithmic bias can arise from historical patterns contained in training data, differences in measurement quality among groups, the use of sensitive attributes or the decisions made on the basis of predicted outcomes (Baker & Hawn, 2022).
834: For this reason, the system must not be described as unbiased solely because a sensitive feature appears to have low model importance. Prior to operational use, the final model should be examined for subgroup performance differences where ethically and legally appropriate, particularly across available attributes such as sex and school. Decision-makers should also assess whether sensitive attributes are necessary to achieve the system's support-oriented purpose or whether excluding them would reduce ethical risk without materially weakening predictive utility.
835: 3.9.3 Responsible Use of the Predictive System
836: Predictions generated by EduPredict AI were intended to initiate supportive academic review. Where a result indicates possible poor performance or academic risk, appropriate actions may include consultation with a teacher or academic adviser, discussion of study strategies, assessment of attendance challenges and referral to available educational support. The prediction should be treated as an indicator requiring contextual review rather than as a final evaluation of personal capability.
837: Responsible use also requires explainability appropriate to the end user. Academic staff should understand the kind of data used, the meaning of the predicted outcome, the limits of the training population and the uncertainty inherent in model predictions. Where a student may be affected by a support recommendation, the decision should remain subject to human judgement and should be capable of being questioned or reviewed, consistent with ethical principles for learning analytics (Slade & Prinsloo, 2013).
838: 3.9.4 Contextual Limitation and Deployment Safeguards
839: The model-development data originated from Portuguese secondary schools; therefore, the system cannot be treated as a validated predictor for students in Nigerian universities without additional evidence. Before any local operational deployment, the system should be retrained or externally validated with data that reflect the intended institutional population, educational grading structure and support practices.

859: 3.10 Chapter Summary
860: This chapter has described the methodology for developing EduPredict AI using the UCI Student Performance dataset. It established the quantitative predictive and system development design, identified the secondary-school population represented by the records, described the combination of the Mathematics and Portuguese language datasets, specified the original G3 academic outcome and outlined the data preparation, modelling, evaluation, deployment and ethical procedures relevant to the prototype. The chapter also identified important constraints concerning prediction timing, sensitive attributes, data privacy and external validity. The following chapter should present the implementation details and empirical model results using only the final verified training pipeline and its recorded output.
